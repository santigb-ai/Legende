Mother's side · Great-grandparents

Fleeing the Rural Crisis

▼
Aragón→ Murcia→ Barcelona

My great-grandparents were part of Spain's great rural exodus — one of the largest internal migrations in European history. From the 1940s onwards, the traditional economies of Aragón and Murcia collapsed. Subsistence farming could no longer support families. Barcelona's booming industrial sector promised wages, housing, and a future. They weren't alone — millions of Spaniards made the same journey north to Catalonia.

Father's side · Great-grandparents & Grandparents

From the South to Catalonia

▼
Andalucía→ Málaga→ Lérida→ Barcelona

My father's family followed a similar path — the pull of Catalonia was impossible to resist. Andalucía, despite its beauty, offered little economic mobility. Lérida became a stepping stone — a Catalan city that offered work in agriculture and industry while being closer to the opportunities of Barcelona. This gradual northward drift was common for southern Spanish families in the mid-20th century, moving in stages rather than all at once.

Both sides · Parents · 2004

Two Journeys, One Destination

▼
Barcelona→ Opernhaus Zürich→ Winterthur→ Thalwil

By 2004, migration looked nothing like it had for my great-grandparents. The EU–Switzerland Bilateral Agreements had opened borders in a way previous generations could never have imagined — making it straightforward for people to move across Europe simply for a better life or a better opportunity. This was the era of skilled, voluntary migration: people choosing where to live, not fleeing from hardship. My parents were part of this quieter, modern wave — two people from Barcelona settling in the Zürich Opernhaus district, eventually making their home in Thalwil, where the long journey of two Spanish families finally came to rest.
